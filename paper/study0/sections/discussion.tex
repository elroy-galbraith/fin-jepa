\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{FT-Transformer as a Financial Encoder}
\label{sec:disc_encoder}

The tuned FT-Transformer beats XGBoost on all four distress outcomes, with the largest gains on rare-event outcomes (\texttt{sec\_enforcement} $+0.075$, \texttt{bankruptcy} $+0.034$, \texttt{earnings\_restate} $+0.033$). It trails LR-full on \texttt{stock\_decline} (0.667 vs.\ 0.682), the highest-prevalence outcome. This aligns with findings from \citet{gorishniy2021revisiting} that attention-based models handle heterogeneous feature interactions more effectively than tree ensembles when the signal is distributed across many features rather than concentrated in a few dominant splits. For distress prediction, where the relevant patterns may involve subtle multi-feature interactions (e.g., deteriorating liquidity ratios combined with rising leverage and falling operating margins), the FT-Transformer's global self-attention may capture dependencies that tree-based models miss.

The FT-Transformer also shows strong calibration on low-base-rate outcomes. On \texttt{sec\_enforcement} (ECE 0.220 vs.\ LR-traditional's 0.456) and \texttt{bankruptcy} (ECE 0.381 vs.\ 0.437), the Transformer produces substantially better-calibrated probabilities despite similar discrimination. In a risk management context, well-calibrated probabilities are arguably more important than marginal discrimination gains, since they enable principled threshold-setting and portfolio-level risk aggregation.

\subsection{Self-Supervised Pretraining}
\label{sec:disc_ssl}

SSL pretraining via masked feature reconstruction provides meaningful gains, with the most dramatic improvement on \texttt{sec\_enforcement} ($+0.093$ AUROC at $r = 0.50$). This outcome has the lowest base rate and the weakest supervised signal---precisely the regime where pretraining should help most, since the encoder can learn financial structure from the full unlabeled dataset rather than relying on scarce positive examples.

The absence of a single optimal mask ratio across outcomes suggests that different outcomes depend on different structural properties of the financial data. Low mask ratios (0.15) may encourage the encoder to learn fine-grained local dependencies between related features (e.g., within the balance sheet), while high mask ratios (0.50) force it to learn more global, abstract relationships. The latter appears more useful for outcomes like \texttt{sec\_enforcement} that may depend on higher-order patterns not captured by any individual financial ratio.

\subsection{Implications for Study~1}
\label{sec:disc_study1}

With the tuned FT-Transformer passing the go/no-go gate (4/4 outcomes, statistically significant on 2/4), the results support proceeding with the FT-Transformer as the financial modality encoder for the JEPA architecture in Study~1. Several findings inform the design:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{SSL pretraining is beneficial.} The encoder should be initialized from SSL-pretrained weights. While no single mask ratio dominates, $r = 0.50$ yields the strongest gains on the most challenging outcomes (\texttt{sec\_enforcement}, \texttt{earnings\_restate}).

  \item \textbf{The latent space captures financial structure.} SSL pretraining improves downstream prediction even though the pretraining objective (feature reconstruction) has no direct relationship to distress labels. This is an encouraging signal: if the latent space is rich enough to support feature reconstruction, it may also be rich enough to support temporal prediction (Study~1) and cross-modal alignment (Study~2).

  \item \textbf{Hyperparameter sensitivity is moderate.} The grid search (\Cref{fig:sweep_heatmap}) shows that performance varies by up to 0.05 mean AUROC across configurations, with learning rate the most influential factor. This suggests that per-study tuning is warranted but the architecture is not fragile.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. The FT-Transformer was tuned via grid search while baselines use default hyperparameters, which may overstate the Transformer's relative advantage; a fairer comparison would tune all models equally. The \texttt{sec\_enforcement} and \texttt{bankruptcy} outcomes have very low base rates (AUPRC $< 0.02$ across all models), making AUROC comparisons noisy---the bootstrap CIs on these outcomes are wide and do not exclude zero. All experiments use a single random seed (42); future work should report variance across multiple seeds. Finally, the test period (2020--2023) spans the COVID-19 market disruption, which provides a valuable out-of-distribution stress test but also means the test distribution may differ systematically from the training distribution in ways that advantage or disadvantage particular model families.
